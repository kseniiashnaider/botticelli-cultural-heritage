Conclusion

The project demonstrates a complete Knowledge Engineering workflow. Knowledge gaps were not assumed but objectively identified through SPARQL exploration. Large Language Models were subsequently employed as knowledge extraction tools rather than authoritative sources. Their outputs were compared, validated and transformed into RDF triples that enrich the cultural heritage description of Botticelli's artwork. The resulting methodology is transparent, reproducible and transferable to other cultural heritage objects. Most importantly, the project shows that semantic enrichment should be evidence- based: existing metadata must first be explored, genuine knowledge gaps identified, generated knowledge validated and only then incorporated into a knowledge graph.

This project achieved its objective of identifying, validating and enriching semantic knowledge gaps in the ArCo Knowledge Graph. Rather than assuming that information was missing, each gap was demonstrated through SPARQL exploration. Large Language Models generated contextual knowledge that was subsequently validated and transformed into RDF triples. The final workflow is transparent, reproducible and suitable for reuse in other cultural heritage knowledge engineering projects. It illustrates that semantic enrichment is most effective when knowledge graphs and LLMs are combined in a carefully controlled methodology.

This chapter demonstrated how semantic knowledge extracted from Large Language Models can be transformed into structured RDF triples for the selected cultural object, Madonna con Bambino, San Giovannino e angeli. The proposed enrichment addresses the knowledge gaps identified during the SPARQL analysis by introducing semantic descriptions of iconography, symbolism, theology and Renaissance cultural context. Although the proposed RDF model is illustrative, it demonstrates a practical workflow for enriching cultural heritage knowledge graphs while remaining consistent with Semantic Web principles.
